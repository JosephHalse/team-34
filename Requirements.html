Requirements

Introduction to Project Background and Client:

Working with our client, NTT Data we were tasked with making an AR application for use at Great Ormond Street Children’s Hospital that could be of use to all kinds of other medical staff and amateurs, making the engagement of technology and modern equipment un-daunting and accessible, merging object recognition with augmented reality displays to easily access information via smart glasses.

NTT Data: The Client

NTT Data was recognised in 2012 as the 5th largest IT service company globally [1]. Originating in Japan in 1967 it became a subsidiary of Nippon Telegraph and Telephone (NTT) in 1988 as its Data Communications division. [2] Now, with headquarters in Japan, NTT data is an international company providing IT services worldwide, with more than 100,000 professionals in over 50 countries. [3] They pride themselves on innovation in finding solutions for business client’s IT needs. We are liaising with Praveen Selvaraj the Delivery Manager of Innovation at their UK London office who has a background in software design and development.

Great Ormond Street Hospital: The User

Founded in 1852 as the first children’s hospital in the English speaking world [4] this pioneering children’s hospital in Bloomsbury, London, has a long association with UCL. [5] Working with The UCL Great Ormond Street Institute of Child Health (ICH) they form the largest concentration of children's health research in Europe. [6] Historic medical breakthroughs at Great Ormond Street Hospital (GOSH) have included the first children’s heart and lung bypass machine (in 1962), the first rubella vaccine and bone marrow transplant. [7]



 It now operates as an NHS trust with much financial input from its own charitable trust, which famously still includes copyright royalties from the story “Peter Pan”, which was donated in 1929 by the author J.M. Barrie to the hospital and is commemorated by a statue of the titular character in the hospital grounds. [8]

A new unit at GOSH is being opened, called DRIVE – Digital Research, Informatics and Virtual Environments, which aims to use the latest technologies to enhance the experience of patients and staff, in partnership with UCL and industry experts in technology, artificial intelligence and digital innovation. [9] NTT Data are involved, with a belief that investing in innovation in immersive experience technologies like AR, has massive “potential to enhance medical practice”, according to Tom Winstanley, Vice president of Digital & Innovation. [10]

Augmented Reality

Augmented Reality or AR is a technology that superimposes computer generated graphics into a view of the real world. This can have the effect of making the graphics look like part of the natural environment, augmentations to reality, as if they are really there. These can be viewed through a screen, such as the popular game “Pokemon Go”

where a camera takes real time images of the world and displays on top of this characters and/or text that blend in and look, when viewed on the screen like they are really there, a part of the scene in front of the camera. AR applications for use with devises like tablet computers and smart phones are becoming popular for purposes of education, easy access to information or just for fun and entertainment, bringing a new realistic dimension alive for the user who can enjoy a more immersive, hands-on experience to interact with. Holding up a smart phone with an AR app can reveal all kinds of useful hidden information about the real world environment such as shop opening hours, bus timetables and nearby places of interest. The possibilities are endless!

The images can also be viewed on smart glasses: the user wears the glasses and the computer generated images and text, are projected directly onto the glasses, so as to be clearly visible, and superimposed on top of their actual view of the world in front of them! This gives the impression of having a large transparent screen approximately 2 metres in front of you! This technology can be put to all kinds of uses to display information, facts, figures or images. This can become an invaluable tool for all kinds of trainees and professionals, replacing the need to stop work and consult books, the information needed can be instantly brought up before their eyes whilst they work. There are many fun and entertaining possibilities too where AR in smart glasses can inform as well as entertain, enhancing children’s educational experiences such as bringing history to life around them.

Object Recognition

Humans can naturally identify objects by their visual appearance, object recognition is the area of computer vision technology that strives to teach computers to do the same, invaluable for all manner of technologies from driverless cars to robotics. The computer has to first recognise an object then be able to detect it in a picture, identifying multiple items in one view (object detection).   [11]

Techniques of deep learning and machine learning are used to train a program to identify objects. For this project we used the technique of making a 3D model with photogrammetry, then tracking it in 3d space using the inbuilt camera on the smart glass.

Project Goals

By using the state of the art Epsom Smart Glasses linked to our AR app programme, a user can interact with what they see around them. Our goal is that the glasses will recognise medical items which they see in front of them, being able to identify different pieces of specialist equipment and show the user information about them. This will enable a technician to access all the information they need, hands free while working, by looking at text displayed on the glasses screen where it is needed and easiest to reach. Gone are the old days of having to put down what you’re doing and consult an old fashioned printed manual! It can all be to be brought up instantly right in front of your eyes! We aim to develop this so it works smoothly, is easy to understand and operate, for both healthcare professionals and amateurs using technology for home healthcare and first aid. 

Benefits of using the app will be: 

User Friendly

The app should be easy and intuitive to use, quick to learn designed for maximum usability requiring minimal computer skills, becoming almost second nature.

Time Saving

Enables work to be carried out quicker and easier without interruption, invaluable for busy health carers.

Informative

Gives the user a wide range of useful text displayed as augmented reality, as if it’s really there, right in front of them, easy to reference.

Hands-Free

No need to put down tools to look at a manual, the AR display is always right in front of your eyes, keeping hands free to continue work. 

Requirement Gathering

Liaising with our contact at NTT Data we gathered information about what was required of the app, who advised that we speak to staff at GOSH in order to get an idea of the specific types of medical equipment for which to program the app to recognise and respond to. While we are waiting for the response we acquired a selection of common medical equipment with which to use as samples to test the method of creating 3D models to teach the app to recognise. We also ended up purchasing a pair of Epson Moverio Smart Glasses as none were available on loan from UCL. 

We carried out research interviewing potential users to gain feedback about ease of use, the appearance of the user interface and general ideas about how the app should look and operate on the glasses, using one to one conversation and responses to questionnaires showing mock ups of screen shots. This gave us a good idea of what we should be aiming for in our design. We researched into the other similar applications that are available.
 
We looked into the use of TensorFlow, Android Studio, the Epsom Moverio SDK and Photogrammetry.
